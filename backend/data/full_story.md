On September 17, 2015, Ethan Mercer turned twenty years old. It was the same day his mother, Clara Mercer, had died giving birth to him twenty years earlier at Raven Falls Hospital. Ethan had never known her, only the hollow space she left behind. His father, David Mercer, decided they would take a road trip to Raven Falls National Park, where Clara was buried beneath the ancient pines. David believed it was time Ethan saw where his mother rested. They packed the car and set out in the evening, the GPS routing them through the Ashwood Tunnel, a narrow passage carved through the mountain.

As they drove into the Ashwood Tunnel, the lights flickered. Ethan felt a strange pressure build in his ears, like diving too deep into water. The world stretched and colors bled together. David gripped the steering wheel, his knuckles white. Then there was darkness and silence. When the light returned, they emerged from the tunnel into a world that felt the same but was not. The trees were younger. The road was different. A truck barreled toward them, its horn blaring.

The impact was devastating. Ethan woke in Raven Falls Hospital with his memories shattered like broken glass. A nurse told him his name was Victor, as written on a scrap of paper found in his pocket. He did not remember anything else, not Ethan, not David, not the tunnel. David Mercer never woke up. He died from his injuries on September 17, 1995, the very same day his son was born in a room just down the hall.

Victor sat in a cold plastic chair outside the operating room, staring at the swinging doors, waiting for news that would never be good. Nearby, another man sat in a similar chair, his face pale and his hands trembling. He was young, in his early twenties, and he clutched a hospital pager like a lifeline. His name, Victor would later learn, was also David Mercer. A nurse emerged and walked past Victor. She told him his father was gone. A second nurse approached the other David and told him his wife had died during childbirth, but his son had survived. The baby was healthy. The baby was beautiful.

The two men looked at each other. In that shared moment of loss, one losing a father and the other losing a wife, something unexpected happened. David, barely holding himself together, grasped at the only thread of familiarity he could find. The name on Victor's hospital wristband read Mercer. David asked Victor to stay, to work with him, to be his brother. Victor agreed because he had nowhere else to go and no memories to return to. Without papers or ceremony, David Mercer adopted a brother.

Victor lived as David's brother for twenty years, helping raise Ethan as a nephew he never fully understood why he loved so fiercely. But something was wrong. He walked into rooms and felt tugs of recognition, a toy on the floor, a song on the radio, the way David laughed at a joke. Then the predictions started. Victor would say there would be a storm before a single cloud appeared. He warned a neighbor about a car accident before the brakes failed. He knew the winner of the town fair pie contest three weeks in advance. The neighborhood called him the Seer, but Victor did not feel like a prophet. He felt like a man reading a book he had already finished.

On September 17, 2015, Victor woke with a headache like shards of glass behind his eyes. The memories crashed over him like a wave. He was not Victor. He was Ethan. And on this very day, his younger self was packing the car for Raven Falls National Park. He grabbed the phone and dialed. He called Ethan to warn him not to go through the tunnel. The call dropped. It was ignored. It came too late. Victor ran through the streets toward the Ashwood Tunnel, the phone clutched in his hand, his breath ragged. He had to reach them. He had to stop the loop. But the tunnel was already glowing.

Victor's phone was found near the entrance of the Ashwood Tunnel. Of Victor himself, there was no sign. Some believe he entered the tunnel and was thrown back to 1995 to start the loop all over again. Others believe he simply vanished, his purpose fulfilled and his paradox finally resolved.

Before Victor ran to the tunnel, before the memories fully returned, something in him had known. In the fragments between dreams and waking, he had scrawled a single word on a crumpled piece of paper and slipped it into his pocket. Victor. A name for a man who had no other. A clue for the boy who would forget everything. Because somewhere deep inside, even without understanding why, Ethan knew he would need a new name to carry him through the years ahead.

Clara Mercer was buried beneath the ancient pines of Raven Falls National Park, the destination her husband and son never reached. She had been a woman of laughter and warmth, sunlight in human form. Her death in childbirth left a silence that David carried like a stone in his chest for two decades. But in the strange mechanics of the loop, her son grew up watching himself grow up. He loved the boy he once was, protected the man his father became, and lived a life tangled in beautiful and impossible paradox. It was not a tragedy. It was a story that kept telling itself on September 17, every year, forever.
